RZA as Bobby Digital in Stereo

1998
 🔈💻 Owned Gee Street / V2
RZA's debut solo album and the introduction of his hedonistic alter ego Bobby Digital, a cybernetic ladies' man navigating a dystopian New York. Abandoning the gritty sample-based aesthetic of Wu-Tang's first wave, RZA built an entirely keyboard-driven sonic world he called the "digital orchestra" — layered synthesizers, drum machines, and cinematic textures that split opinions on release but now stand as a prescient left turn. The album debuted at No. 16 on the Billboard 200 and was certified gold.

Digital Bullet

2001
Koch Records
The second Bobby Digital album, darker and more aggressive than its predecessor. Released through Koch after RZA departed Gee Street, the album pushed the digital orchestra concept further with heavier production and a more confrontational Bobby Digital persona. It peaked at No. 24 on the Billboard 200, featuring guest appearances from Method Man, ODB, Masta Killa, GZA, and Big Gipp of Goodie Mob. Production was handled primarily by RZA alongside Mathematics, True Master, and Tony Touch.

Digi Snacks

2008
Wu Music / Koch
The third Bobby Digital album, arriving seven years after Digital Bullet and showcasing a mellower, more reflective side of the alter ego. Co-produced extensively with Panauh Kalayeh, the album features an eclectic guest roster including John Frusciante of Red Hot Chili Peppers, George Clinton, El DeBarge, and David Banner. Inspectah Deck contributes a standout verse on "You Can't Stop Me Now," while the Frusciante-assisted "Up Again" marked an unexpected rock-rap fusion.

Bobby Digital and the Pit of Snakes

2022
MNRK Music
The fourth Bobby Digital installment and the first in fourteen years, presented as a multimedia concept tying into RZA's Bobby Digital NFT and graphic novel projects. A lean eight-track affair that revisits the alter ego with a modern production palette, blending trap-adjacent textures with RZA's signature cinematic atmospherics. The album was released alongside an animated companion series and represents Bobby Digital's evolution into the digital age he originally prophesied.

Birth of a Prince

2003
Sanctuary
RZA's sole album under his own name rather than the Bobby Digital persona, and a conscious return to grittier, more boom-bap-oriented production after the digital experiments. Heavy with Wu-Tang family features — Masta Killa, Ghostface Killah, and a posthumous Ol' Dirty Bastard appearance on "We Pop" — the album was conceived as a spiritual counterpart to the Bobby Digital series. Bronze Nazareth emerged as a notable production contributor, while the Cilvaringz collaboration "You'll Never Know" showcased RZA's mentorship of European Wu affiliates.

Notable Soundtracks

2000 · Film scoring work on Razor Sharp / Epic

Ghost Dog: The Way of the Samurai

2000
 💻 In library Razor Sharp / Epic
RZA's first full film score, composed for Jim Jarmusch's existential hitman meditation starring Forest Whitaker. A landmark in hip-hop cinema scoring, the soundtrack weaves together atmospheric instrumentals, Wu-Tang posse cuts, and spoken Hagakure passages by Whitaker into a seamless blend of Eastern philosophy and street-level grit. The score established RZA as a serious film composer, leading to subsequent work on Kill Bill, The Man with the Iron Fists, and beyond. Received wide critical acclaim and later a deluxe vinyl reissue through Sacred Bones.
