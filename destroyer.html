We’ll Build Them a Golden Bridge

1996
Tinker
Sixteen lo-fi home recordings that constitute Bejar’s debut, released on the tiny Vancouver label Tinker Recordings. The songs are skeletal and cassette-rough, but the literary ambition and melodic instincts are already audible. Tracks reference everything from labor politics to country music mythology, sketching a worldview that would deepen across the next three decades. A cult document more than a polished statement, later reissued on Scratch Records in 2006.

City of Daughters

1998
Triple Crown
The first proper studio effort, produced by John Collins at JC/DC Studios in Vancouver — beginning a partnership that would define the Destroyer sound for decades. The seventeen-track album is dense and ambitious, weaving literary references, synth textures, and Collins’s careful production into something far more expansive than the debut. The songs-within-songs structure and self-referential lyrical world that would become Bejar’s signature are already crystallizing here.

Thief

2000
 🔈💻 Owned Catsup Plate
The third album and a significant step forward, bringing Jason Zumpano’s keyboards and Stephen Wood’s layered guitars into the fold alongside Collins and Morgan. The result is lusher and more cinematic than anything before, with songs like “Destroyer’s the Temple” and the sprawling title track showcasing Bejar’s growing confidence as an arranger. Released on the American indie label Catsup Plate, it marks the beginning of Destroyer’s crossover from local Vancouver concern to wider underground recognition.

Streethawk: A Seduction

2001
 🔈💻 Owned Misra
The breakthrough. Recorded at JC/DC Studios in the fall of 2000, this is the album where everything snaps into focus — Bejar’s literary-glam ambitions, the band’s increasingly sophisticated arrangements, and an irresistible sense of romantic grandiosity. Songs like “The Bad Arts” and “Beggars Might Ride” toggle between intimacy and anthemic sweep, while the bookending “Streethawk” instrumentals frame the album as a conceptual whole. Widely regarded as one of the great Canadian indie records of its era.

This Night

2002
 🔈💻 Owned Merge
Bejar’s first release on Merge Records and a deliberate swerve toward sprawling, loosely recorded maximalism. A new lineup — Nicolas Bragg on guitar, Chris Frey on bass, Fisher Rose on drums — replaced the Streethawk band, and the fifteen tracks revel in beautiful chaos: trumpet fanfares, organ drones, and Bejar’s increasingly theatrical vocal delivery. Critics were divided, some hearing purposeful mess, others a visionary expansion. Time has been kind to it.

Your Blues

2004
 🔈💻 Owned Merge
An audacious left turn: Bejar replaced his live band with MIDI-generated orchestration, coining the term “European blues” for the result. Lush, theatrical, and deliberately artificial, the album sounds like a lost Europop opera scored for synthesized strings and brass. The songs themselves — “Notorious Lightning,” “An Actor’s Revenge,” “Don’t Become the Thing You Hated” — are among his most melodically inventive. A polarizing record that rewarded patient listeners and signaled that no two Destroyer albums would ever sound alike.

Destroyer’s Rubies

2006
 🔈💻 Owned Merge
The consensus masterpiece. Bejar returned to a full live band after the MIDI experiment of Your Blues, and the result is his most confident and expansive record — ten songs of surging, literate indie rock anchored by the nine-minute opening title track. Nicolas Bragg’s guitar work, Ted Bois’s keyboards, and Fisher Rose’s vibraphone give the album a warm, muscular richness. It became Destroyer’s best-selling album to that point and remains the entry point most fans recommend.

Trouble in Dreams

2008
 🔈💻 Owned Merge
A direct sequel to the Rubies-era band sound, recorded at JC/DC in the summer of 2007 with the same core ensemble. Eleven tracks of tightly wound, guitar-driven art-rock that maintains the predecessor’s energy while adding new textures — Rodney Graham guests on gong and potato. “Blue Flower/Blue Flame” and “Foam Hands” are standouts, and the album as a whole represents the peak of Destroyer’s mid-period guitar-band incarnation before Bejar would pivot dramatically with Kaputt.

Kaputt

2011
 🔈💻 Owned Merge Dead Oceans
The reinvention that changed everything. Bejar abandoned guitar rock entirely for a lush, soft-rock/sophisti-pop palette of saxophones, trumpet, synthesizers, and drum machines — channeling Roxy Music, Steely Dan, and late-night FM radio through his unmistakable lyrical sensibility. “Chinatown” and “Kaputt” became the closest things Destroyer had to hits, and the album landed on dozens of year-end lists. It proved Bejar could execute a total sonic reinvention while keeping his songwriting identity intact.

Poison Season

2015
 🔈 In collection Merge Dead Oceans
A dramatic, orchestral affair that pulls back from Kaputt’s soft-rock elegance toward something grander and more cinematic. Bookended by two versions of “Times Square, Poison Season” performed with a string quintet, the album moves through Springsteen-scaled rock (“Dream Lover”), torch-song balladry (“Archer on the Beach”), and horn-drenched art-pop. It’s Bejar’s most ambitious production, with a large ensemble of horns, strings, and guest vocalists filling out the arrangements.

ken

2017
 💻 In library Merge
The darkest, most atmospheric Destroyer album. Drummer Joshua Wells served as producer for the first time, and the result is dense, goth-inflected art-pop built on layers of synthesizer, drum programming, and reverb-heavy guitar. Bejar described it as the most goth record anyone would hear from him. “Tinseltown Swimming in Blood” is a nocturnal highlight, and the album as a whole trades the gregariousness of Kaputt and Poison Season for something colder and more interior.

Have We Met

2020
 🔈 In collection Merge Dead Oceans
A lean, ten-song set built largely from John Collins’s laptop and iPad experiments — synth textures, drum programming, and guitar lines from Nicolas Bragg layered into surreal, nocturnal pop. The production is more minimal and electronic than anything since Your Blues, but warmer and more inviting. “Crimson Tide” and “Cue Synthesizer” are among Bejar’s catchiest songs, and the album was widely praised as one of his most accessible and emotionally direct.

LABYRINTHITIS

2022
 🔈 In collection Merge Bella Union
Bejar and Collins made this album remotely during the pandemic, sending ideas back and forth between Vancouver and Galiano Island. The result is Destroyer at its most playful and disorienting: club-adjacent beats, distorted synths, and tape-loop textures collide with Bejar’s stream-of-consciousness lyrics. “June” and “It’s in Your Heart Now” are hypnotic extended pieces, while “Tintoretto, It’s for You” distills the album’s restless energy into three brisk minutes. A late-career curveball that proved Bejar still had new territory to explore.

Dan’s Boogie

2025
 🔈 In collection Merge
The fourteenth album finds Bejar playing a lounge singer and hustler, imagining himself as a supporting character in his own fantasies. Nine songs that lean into piano-driven balladry, wry romanticism, and a warmth that feels earned after three decades of reinvention. “Bologna” features guest vocals from Simone Schmidt of Fiver, and Joseph Shabason returns on saxophone for the first time since Poison Season. Collins again produces, and the album sounds like a late-night cabaret — intimate, slightly dissolute, and unmistakably Destroyer.
