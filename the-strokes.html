Is This It

2001
 🔈💻 Owned RCA
The album that rebooted rock at the start of the new century. Recorded at Transporterraum studio in downtown Manhattan with producer Gordon Raphael, Is This It stripped guitar music to its nervy, melodic essentials. Casablancas's buried-in-the-mix vocals, Valensi and Hammond Jr.'s interlocking guitar lines, and the impossibly tight rhythm section created a sound both retro and urgent. Initially released in Australia on Rough Trade before RCA issued it worldwide, it became the critical consensus album of 2001 and a generational touchstone for garage rock revival.

Room on Fire

2003
 🔈💻 Owned RCA
The difficult second album that turned out to be a worthy companion to the debut. Gordon Raphael returned to produce, this time at Transporterraum's new Long Island City location, preserving the lo-fi grit while subtly expanding the palette. The guitars are sharper and more layered, Casablancas's melodies more adventurous. Singles like "Reptilia" and "12:51" demonstrated range, while "Under Control" and "The End Has No End" revealed a maturing band unafraid of vulnerability. Commercially strong but overshadowed by the debut's mythology, it has aged gracefully into a fan favorite.

First Impressions of Earth

2006
 💻 In library RCA
The pivot point. Swapping Gordon Raphael for David Kahne (who replaced Raphael midway through sessions), the band pushed toward a bigger, heavier sound across fourteen tracks — their longest album by far. The guitars gained distortion and density, Casablancas's vocals moved to the foreground, and the songwriting grew more ambitious and uneven in equal measure. "Juicebox" and "Heart in a Cage" hit hard; "Ask Me Anything" stripped everything back to synths and voice. Divisive on release, it revealed a band restlessly unsatisfied with repeating itself.

Angles

2011
RCA
Born from a fractured recording process — Casablancas largely absent from the studio, sending vocal parts remotely — Angles arrived after a five-year gap as the band's most stylistically varied record. Joe Chiccarelli and Gus Oberg shared production duties, and the sound veered from new wave synths to Beatlesque pop to funk-inflected grooves. "Under Cover of Darkness" proved the old magic was intact; "Machu Picchu" and "Taken for a Fool" showed new colors. The collaborative songwriting marked a departure from Casablancas's prior dominance, with mixed but intriguing results.

Comedown Machine

2013
RCA
The most underrated entry in their catalog, and the last album under their RCA contract. Produced by Gus Oberg, Comedown Machine leans into falsetto vocals, synth textures, and a restrained, almost introverted energy that bewildered fans expecting garage rock bombast. "One Way Trigger" channels a-ha; "Tap Out" is wistful pop; "80's Comedown Machine" drifts into ambient territory. Barely promoted and released without a tour, it was initially dismissed but has since been critically reappraised as a quietly adventurous record that proved the band could evolve beyond their brand.

The New Abnormal

2020
Cult RCA
The triumphant return, seven years after Comedown Machine and produced by Rick Rubin at his Shangri-La studio in Malibu. Longer, looser, and more emotionally exposed than anything in their catalog, The New Abnormal gave the band room to breathe — tracks stretch past five and six minutes without losing momentum. "The Adults Are Talking" and "Bad Decisions" recapture the band's rhythmic swagger, while "At the Door" and "Ode to the Mets" are among Casablancas's most nakedly affecting vocal performances. It won the Grammy for Best Rock Album, a belated institutional acknowledgment of the band's significance.
