Radiohead

From grunge-adjacent also-rans to one of the most important rock bands of their generation. Across nine albums, Radiohead systematically dismantled and rebuilt their sound — moving from guitar rock through electronic experimentation to a singular synthesis that defined the anxieties of the digital age.

Early Era

1993–1995 · From "Creep" to Credibility

Pablo Honey

1993
Parlophone / Capitol UK #22 · US #32
Radiohead's debut arrived in the thick of grunge and was often dismissed as derivative, but "Creep" — a self-lacerating anthem of alienation — became a worldwide hit that both launched and haunted the band. The rest of the album is raw, guitar-heavy, and uneven, though "Anyone Can Play Guitar" and "Blow Out" hint at the ambition to come. Certified platinum in the UK and US.
Produced by
Sean Slade, Paul Q. Kolderie

The Bends

1995
Parlophone / Capitol UK #4 · Triple Platinum UK
The album that transformed Radiohead from one-hit wonders into critical darlings. Jonny Greenwood's layered, inventive guitar work gives songs like "Fake Plastic Trees," "Just," and "Street Spirit (Fade Out)" an emotional density that transcended Britpop. The songwriting is vastly more sophisticated than the debut — Yorke's lyrics traded self-pity for something more cinematic and existential. A modern classic of '90s guitar rock.
Produced by
John Leckie

OK Computer

1997 · The Masterpiece

OK Computer

1997
Parlophone / Capitol UK #1 · US #21 · Triple Platinum
Radiohead's defining statement and one of the most acclaimed albums of the 1990s. A sprawling, anxious meditation on technology, alienation, and modern life, built from towering guitar architecture, orchestral textures, and Yorke's increasingly fragmented vocals. "Paranoid Android," "Karma Police," and "No Surprises" became generation-defining singles. Produced largely at a mansion in Bath and at Abbey Road, its cinematic ambition expanded what rock music could attempt. Regularly cited among the greatest albums ever recorded.

The Electronic Turn

2000–2003 · Dismantling Guitar Rock

Kid A

2000
Parlophone / Capitol UK #1 · US #1
The most radical left turn in modern rock history. Influenced by Aphex Twin, Autechre, and Warp Records, Radiohead abandoned conventional song structures for fragmented electronic textures, warped vocals, and dissonant orchestration. "Everything in Its Right Place" opens with a Fender Rhodes loop that signals: the old band is gone. "Idioteque" channels millennial dread through a skittering beat. Polarizing on release, now widely considered among the greatest albums of the 2000s.
Produced by
Nigel Godrich, Radiohead
electronic art rock experimental IDM-influenced

Amnesiac

2001
Parlophone / Capitol UK #1 · US #2
Recorded during the same sessions as Kid A but not simply a collection of leftovers. Where Kid A felt cold and abstracted, Amnesiac is more unsettled and organic — jazz inflections creep in on "Life in a Glasshouse" (featuring Humphrey Lyttelton's band), while "Pyramid Song" is among Yorke's most devastating ballads. "I Might Be Wrong" reconnects with guitar rock, if only briefly.
Produced by
Nigel Godrich, Radiohead
art rock electronic experimental

Hail to the Thief

2003
Parlophone / Capitol UK #1 · US #3 · Platinum
A partial return to guitar-driven rock, charged with anxiety about the Iraq War and the Bush administration. Radiohead's longest album at 56 minutes and 14 tracks — Yorke later said he wished they'd trimmed it. But the best tracks hit hard: "2 + 2 = 5" is a furious opener, "There There" builds from a tribal pulse into a towering guitar climax, and "A Wolf at the Door" is a paranoid spoken-word fever dream. Their final album for EMI.
Produced by
Nigel Godrich, Radiohead

The Independent Era

2007–2016 · Self-Released and XL Recordings

In Rainbows

2007
Self-released / XL UK #1 · US #1 · 3M+ copies
Released as a pay-what-you-want download — a move that made as many headlines as the music. But the album itself is arguably Radiohead's warmest and most emotionally direct since The Bends, full of gorgeous textures and Yorke's most inviting melodies. "Reckoner" shimmers, "Nude" aches, "15 Step" grooves in odd time. A synthesis of everything they'd learned: the experimentation of Kid A delivered with newfound intimacy. Widely considered their best work alongside OK Computer.

Nigel Godrich, Radiohead

The King of Limbs

2011
Self-released / XL UK #7 · US #3
Radiohead's shortest and most rhythmically obsessive album — eight tracks built from looped percussion, sampled beats, and heavily processed textures. "Bloom" is a shimmering polyrhythmic web; "Lotus Flower" (with its iconic Yorke-dancing video) is the closest thing to a pop single. Divisive among fans — some find it slight, others hear a richly layered headphone album that reveals itself slowly. Broke their streak of four consecutive UK #1 albums.
Produced by
Nigel Godrich, Radiohead
electronic art rock loop-based

A Moon Shaped Pool

2016
XL Recordings UK #1 · US #3
Radiohead's most orchestral and emotionally raw album, shaped by Yorke's separation from his partner of 23 years and by Jonny Greenwood's film-scoring experience. The London Contemporary Orchestra provides lush string arrangements; "Burn the Witch" bristles with col legno tension. "True Love Waits" — a fan favorite played live since 1995 — finally receives its studio version as a devastating, stripped-back piano closer. As of 2026, their most recent studio album.
Produced by
Nigel Godrich

Live Albums

Official Live Releases

I Might Be Wrong: Live Recordings

2001
Parlophone / Capitol
Seven tracks from the Kid A/Amnesiac tour, plus "True Love Waits" — then available only as this haunting solo acoustic performance. Captures the challenge and triumph of translating the studio experiments into a live band context. "The National Anthem" in particular gains ferocity on stage.
live electronic

In Rainbows – From the Basement

2008
Self-released
A live-in-studio performance of the complete In Rainbows album for Nigel Godrich's "From the Basement" series. No audience, no overdubs — just the five members playing the album front to back. Often cited as the definitive performance of this material, with a clarity and directness that even the studio album doesn't quite match.
