PJ Harvey

Polly Jean Harvey — one of the most fearless and shape-shifting artists in rock, whose three-decade career has moved from the raw blues-punk fury of the early trio through gothic Americana, spectral piano ballads, and politically charged art-rock, reinventing herself completely with each record while maintaining an uncompromising artistic vision.

Dry

1992
Too Pure
Harvey's debut arrived fully formed, a jagged manifesto of desire, frustration, and female power delivered by her eponymous trio. Recorded at The Icehouse in Yeovil with co-producer Head (Howard Bullivant), the album channels blues and post-punk into something rawer than either. "Sheela-Na-Gig" and "Dress" became underground anthems, while deep cuts like "Plants and Rags" and "Water" revealed a songwriter already operating with remarkable range and control.

Rid of Me

1993
 💻 In library Island
Harvey's major-label debut and her most abrasive record, produced by Steve Albini at Pachyderm Studio in Minnesota with his trademark dry, visceral sound. The dynamics are extreme — whispers exploding into screams, silence detonating into walls of noise. "50ft Queenie" and the title track are among the most physically intense songs in the alternative rock canon, while the Bob Dylan cover "Highway '61 Revisited" is gleefully unhinged. Ranked among the greatest albums of the 1990s.

To Bring You My Love

1995
 💻 In library Island
A dramatic reinvention that traded the trio's raw power for a cinematic, blues-gothic landscape. Co-produced with Flood and John Parish at Townhouse Studios in London, the album introduced a far more expansive and theatrical Harvey, with organ drones, string arrangements, and layered atmospherics. "Down by the Water" became her biggest single, and the record earned a Grammy nomination, establishing her as one of the most important artists of the decade.

Is This Desire?

1998
 💻 In library Island
Harvey's most introverted and electronically textured album, a stark departure from the theatrical grandeur of its predecessor. Again co-produced with Flood, the record trades blues swagger for claustrophobic loops, sampled percussion, and eerie digital processing. Character sketches of damaged women — Catherine, Angelene, Leah — populate a nocturnal world of longing and isolation. Commercially cooler on arrival, it has aged into one of her most admired works.

Stories from the City, Stories from the Sea

2000
 💻 In library Island
Harvey's most outward-looking and emotionally direct album, inspired by her time living in New York City and written during a period of personal contentment rare in her catalog. The production by Rob Ellis, Mick Harvey, and Harvey herself is warm and guitar-driven, recalling classic rock with a modern edge. "This Mess We're In," a duet with Thom Yorke, and "Good Fortune" became fan favorites. The album won the Mercury Prize in 2001, confirming her status as a singular force.

Uh Huh Her

2004
 💻 In library Island
A return to raw, guitar-driven intensity after the relative polish of its predecessor. Harvey played nearly every instrument herself and co-produced with longtime engineer Head, making it her most hands-on solo effort since the early trio days. Rob Ellis returned on drums. The sound is loose and combustible, full of jagged riffs and vocal distortion, with "The Letter," "Shame," and "You Come Through" standing out as immediate highlights. A deliberate stripping-back of the orchestral ambitions of the previous decade.

White Chalk

2007
 💻 In library Island
Harvey's most radical transformation: a hushed, piano-led album sung almost entirely in a fragile high soprano, a world away from the guitar-wielding howl of her earlier work. Drawing on the landscape and folklore of her native Dorset, the songs evoke Victorian ghost stories and chalk-white cliffs, suffused with themes of death, loss, and haunting. "When Under Ether" and "The Devil" are among her most unsettling and beautiful compositions. A divisive record on release, now widely regarded as one of her finest.

Let England Shake

2011
 💻 In library Island
A sweeping meditation on England, war, and nationhood that won Harvey her second Mercury Prize — making her the only artist to win the award twice. Recorded in a Dorset church with longtime collaborators John Parish, Mick Harvey, and drummer Jean-Marc Butty, the album's autoharp-driven songs draw on World War I, Gallipoli, and the erosion of English identity. Samples of older recordings weave through the fabric, and Harvey's vocals float above like dispatches from the front. Widely hailed as a masterpiece.

The Hope Six Demolition Project

2016
Island
Inspired by Harvey's travels to Afghanistan, Kosovo, and Washington D.C., this is her most overtly political album since Let England Shake, turning outward to document communities under pressure. Recorded at Somerset House in London as a public art installation — visitors watched through one-way glass — and produced with Flood and John Parish. The same core band returns, augmented by a community choir and horns. "The Community of Hope" and "The Orange Monkey" are pointed dispatches from the world's neglected corners.

I Inside the Old Year Dying

2023
Partisan
Harvey's most recent album and first in seven years, adapted from her poetry collection Orlam, written in Dorset dialect. A deeply pastoral and personal work, it returns to the landscape of her childhood — the fields, rivers, and folklore of southwest England — filtered through incantatory, almost trance-like songwriting. Produced with Flood and John Parish, it is her quietest and most literary record, with Harvey, Parish, and longtime associate Cecil layering acoustic instruments over subtle electronic textures.

With John Parish · 1996 & 2009

Dance Hall at Louse Point

1996
with John Parish Island
The first collaboration between Harvey and her fellow Dorset musician John Parish, credited to "John Parish & Polly Jean Harvey." Parish composed all the music and played most instruments; Harvey wrote lyrics and sang. The result is darker and more abstract than either artist's solo work, with cinematic instrumentals, spoken-word passages, and unsettling atmospherics. "That Was My Veil" and "Taut" are highlights of this adventurous, under-appreciated record.

A Woman a Man Walked By

2009
with John Parish Island
The second Parish-Harvey collaboration, this time credited to "PJ Harvey & John Parish," reversing the billing. Again, Parish wrote the music and Harvey the words, but the sound is wilder and more confrontational than its predecessor. "Black Hearted Love" is one of their most accessible songs together, while "Pig Will Not" and "Passionless, Pointless" push into genuinely abrasive territory. An uncompromising record that rewards repeated listening.
