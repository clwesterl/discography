Trumpet · Composer · Bandleader  ·  1926–1991

Miles Davis

The most restlessly innovative figure in jazz history, whose career traced an unbroken arc of reinvention from bebop through cool jazz, hard bop, modal jazz, post-bop, jazz-rock fusion, and funk — each phase redefining what the music could be and who could listen to it.

Birth of the Cool

1949–1950 · The Nonet Sessions

Birth of the Cool

1957
In library Capitol Rec. Jan & Apr 1949, Mar 1950
Compiled from three nonet sessions that rewrote the rules of jazz arranging. Gil Evans, Gerry Mulligan, and John Lewis wrote charts for an unconventional instrumentation — French horn, tuba, and only six horns total — producing a chamber-jazz cool that countered bebop's heat. The 24-year-old Davis's understated trumpet became the voice of a new aesthetic.

Prestige Records

1954–1956 · Hard Bop Foundations & the Marathon Sessions

Walkin'

1957
Prestige Rec. Apr 3 & 29, 1954 · Van Gelder Studio
A compilation of two 1954 sessions that marked Davis's emergence from his early-50s struggles. The title track is a 13-minute blues masterclass that essentially launched hard bop as a movement. With J.J. Johnson and Lucky Thompson on the A-side, and a leaner quintet with David Schildkraut on the B-side, Davis sounds reborn — confident, lyrical, and swinging.

Bags' Groove

1957
Prestige Rec. Jun 29 & Dec 24, 1954 · Van Gelder Studio
Features the legendary Christmas Eve 1954 session with Thelonious Monk on piano — the source of the famous dispute over whether Monk should comp behind Davis's solos (he didn't). The music itself is relaxed and authoritative, with Milt Jackson's vibes adding warmth. The June session with Sonny Rollins is equally superb.

Cookin' with the Miles Davis Quintet

1957
Prestige Rec. Oct 26, 1956 · Van Gelder Studio
The first released of the four legendary marathon-session albums, featuring the First Great Quintet. Opens with a definitive "My Funny Valentine" and includes a blistering "Blues by Five." The quintet's telepathic interplay set the standard for small-group jazz — Red Garland's block chords, Chambers's singing bass, Philly Joe's crisp drive.

1958
Prestige Rec. May 11 & Oct 26, 1956 · Van Gelder Studio
Perhaps the most beloved of the marathon session albums. "If I Were a Bell" swings with effortless joy, and Coltrane's solo on "Oleo" already hints at the torrential style to come. The informal, first-take atmosphere captures a working band at the peak of its powers — loose, confident, and supremely musical.

Coltrane's harmonic adventurousness is increasingly evident, and the contrast with Davis's cool restraint is endlessly productive.

First Great Quintet & Sextet — Columbia

1955–1961 · The Classic Small-Group Records

'Round About Midnight

1957
Columbia Rec. Oct 1955–Sep 1956 · Columbia 30th Street Studio
Davis's Columbia debut and the first document of the First Great Quintet on that label. The Harmon-muted reading of Monk's title tune became one of jazz's most iconic sounds. Coltrane was still finding his voice as a soloist, but the rhythm section — Garland, Chambers, Jones — is already a machine of swing and sensitivity.

Milestones

1958
Columbia Rec. Feb & Mar 1958 · Columbia 30th Street Studio
The bridge between hard bop and modal jazz. The title track — built on two scales rather than chord changes — pointed directly toward Kind of Blue. With Cannonball Adderley expanding the quintet to a sextet, the frontline has unusual depth. "Straight, No Chaser" burns with the authority of a band that has been playing these tunes every night.

Kind of Blue

1959
In library Columbia Rec. Mar 2 & Apr 22, 1959 · Columbia 30th Street Studio
The best-selling jazz album of all time and a watershed in American music. Davis gave the sextet modal sketches — scales and brief melodic ideas rather than chord sequences — and captured almost everything in single takes. Bill Evans's impressionistic piano replaced Garland's, transforming the group's harmonic palette. Every track is a standard.

Someday My Prince Will Come

1961
Columbia Rec. Mar 1961 · Columbia 30th Street Studio
A transitional album between the First and Second Quintets, with Hank Mobley on most tracks and Coltrane guesting on two (their final studio encounter). The waltz-time title track became a Davis staple. Wynton Kelly, Chambers, and Cobb provide a buoyant rhythmic foundation. The cover, featuring Davis's then-wife Frances Taylor, was Columbia's first to depict a Black woman.

Gil Evans Orchestral Collaborations

1957–1960 · The Great Columbia Orchestra Records

Miles Ahead

1957
In library Columbia Rec. May 1957 · Columbia 30th Street Studio
The first Davis-Evans orchestra collaboration, and a landmark in jazz orchestration. Davis plays flugelhorn exclusively, floating over Evans's luminous, seamlessly connected arrangements for a 19-piece ensemble. The suite-like structure — ten pieces flowing into one another — anticipated the concept album by a decade. Evans's voicings remain unlike anything else in jazz.

Porgy and Bess

1959
Columbia Rec. Jul–Aug 1958 · Columbia 30th Street Studio
Davis and Evans's reimagining of Gershwin's opera is arguably their most emotionally direct collaboration. Davis's trumpet — by turns mournful, ecstatic, and aching — carries the weight of the drama while Evans wraps it in orchestral colors of astonishing richness. "Summertime" and "Prayer" are devastating. Cannonball Adderley guests on alto.

Sketches of Spain

1960
In library Columbia Rec. Nov 1959–Mar 1960 · Columbia 30th Street Studio
The most ambitious Davis-Evans collaboration, built around Joaquin Rodrigo's Concierto de Aranjuez for guitar and orchestra, rearranged for trumpet and jazz ensemble. Davis plays with austere, aching beauty over Evans's Spanish-inflected orchestrations. A polarizing album on release — jazz purists objected — but now recognized as one of the great fusions of jazz and classical sensibility.

The Second Great Quintet

1965–1968 · Shorter, Hancock, Carter, Williams

E.S.P.

1965
Columbia Rec. Jan 20–22, 1965
The debut recording of the Second Great Quintet — the youngest, most adventurous rhythm section in jazz. No standards, no ballads: entirely original compositions (five by Shorter) that opened new harmonic and rhythmic territory. The telepathic interplay between these five musicians redefined the possibilities of the jazz quintet format.

Miles Smiles

1967
Columbia Rec. Oct 24–25, 1966 · Columbia 30th Street Studio
The quintet's most advanced acoustic statement. Forms are loosened, meter becomes fluid, and the rhythm section operates with an independence bordering on free jazz while maintaining an underlying pulse. Wayne Shorter's "Footprints" became a jazz standard; Eddie Harris's "Freedom Jazz Dance" became a Davis staple. Tony Williams's drumming is revolutionary throughout.

Sorcerer

1967
Columbia Rec. May 16–24, 1967 · Columbia 30th Street Studio
The most introspective of the Second Quintet records. Shorter contributes five of the seven compositions, each a miniature of oblique beauty. The quintet plays with such restraint and mutual listening that the spaces between notes carry as much meaning as the notes themselves. "Masqualero" became a touchstone of the period.

Nefertiti

1968
In library Columbia Rec. Jun–Jul 1967 · Columbia 30th Street Studio
Davis's last fully acoustic album and the quintet's most radical. The title track inverts jazz convention entirely: the horns repeat the melody in a hypnotic loop while the rhythm section improvises beneath them. It was the final, logical step of the group's exploration of form, and one of the most quietly revolutionary gestures in jazz history.

Filles de Kilimanjaro

1969
Columbia Rec. Jun & Sep 1968 · Columbia 30th Street Studio
The hinge album between acoustic post-bop and the electric era. Three tracks feature the last recordings of the Second Great Quintet; two introduce Chick Corea and Dave Holland as Hancock and Carter's replacements. Hancock plays electric Rhodes piano for the first time on a Davis record. The music is restless, dense, and already reaching toward something new.

The Electric Period

1969–1975 · Fusion, Funk, and the Studio as Instrument

In a Silent Way

1969
In library Columbia Rec. Feb 18, 1969 · Columbia 30th Street Studio
The threshold of fusion — recorded in a single session and sculpted by producer Teo Macero into two side-long suites. Three electric keyboards shimmer and overlap; John McLaughlin's guitar adds a rock edge; the rhythm floats in a groove that seems to have no beginning or end. Beautiful, hypnotic, and revolutionary — it invented ambient jazz before the term existed.
Personnel
Miles Davis (tp), Wayne Shorter (ss), John McLaughlin (el g), Herbie Hancock (el p), Chick Corea (el p), Joe Zawinul (org), Dave Holland (b), Tony Williams (d). Produced by Teo Macero.

Bitches Brew

1970
In library Columbia Rec. Aug 19–21, 1969 · Columbia Studio B
The album that shattered jazz's boundaries and sold half a million copies. Davis assembled a rotating cast of up to 14 musicians — two drummers, two bassists, three keyboards — and Teo Macero edited the sprawling session tapes into a double album of unprecedented scale and intensity. Dark, brooding, and rhythmically relentless, it created jazz-rock fusion as a commercial force.

A Tribute to Jack Johnson

1971
Columbia Rec. Feb & Apr 1970 · Columbia 30th Street Studio
The hardest-rocking album Davis ever made — a documentary soundtrack built from ferocious studio jams. "Right Off" opens with McLaughlin's blazing guitar riff over Michael Henderson's locked-in funk bass, and Davis enters with one of his most electrifying solos. Two side-long tracks of raw, guitar-driven fusion that anticipated punk's energy and hip-hop's sampling aesthetic.

On the Corner

1972
In library Columbia Rec. Jun–Jul 1972 · Columbia Studio E
Davis's most radical studio album — inspired by Sly Stone, James Brown, and Stockhausen in equal measure. Wah-wah trumpet, electric sitar, tablas, and multiple percussion layers create a dense, polyrhythmic funk that alienated both jazz and rock audiences on release. Now widely regarded as visionary: a direct ancestor of hip-hop, go-go, and electronic dance music.

Get Up with It

1974
In library Columbia Rec. 1970–1974 · Various sessions
A double album compiled from sessions spanning four years, and Davis's last studio release before his six-year retirement. The 32-minute "He Loved Him Madly" — a tribute to Duke Ellington — is a sustained ambient drone that Brian Eno cited as a direct influence. "Calypso Frelimo" and "Rated X" are punishing, relentless funk. An underrated masterpiece of range and extremity.

The Comeback

1981–1991 · Pop Fusion, Synths, and the Warner Bros. Years

The Man with the Horn

1981
Columbia Rec. May 1980–May 1981
Davis's return after six years of silence — a period of health crises, addiction, and isolation. The music fuses pop, R&B, and fusion in a style that divided critics but announced his presence to a new generation. "Back Seat Betty" and "Fat Time" have genuine funk power; the playing is leaner and more fragile than the 70s work, but unmistakably Miles.

Star People

1983
Columbia Rec. 1982–1983
The first album to feature guitarist John Scofield, whose wiry, blues-drenched tone became the cornerstone of Davis's 80s band. The last album produced by Teo Macero. The extended blues jams ("Star People," "Star on Cicely") show Davis reconnecting with his blues roots through an 80s electric lens. Gil Evans contributes an arrangement.

1984
Columbia Rec. Jun–Sep 1983
Davis's first album without Teo Macero as producer, and a sharper, more modern-sounding record than its predecessors. Branford Marsalis guests on soprano saxophone, adding a youthful energy. Scofield's guitar is prominently featured on the longer jams. A transitional record that points toward the sleeker Warner Bros. sound to come.

You're Under Arrest

1985
Columbia Rec. 1984–1985
Davis's final Columbia album after a 30-year association. The pop covers — Cyndi Lauper's "Time After Time" and Michael Jackson's "Human Nature" — became concert staples, proving Davis could transform any material into his own. Sting narrates a spoken-word interlude. The album marked the end of an era; Davis moved to Warner Bros. seeking creative freedom.

Tutu

1986
Warner Bros. Rec. 1986
A reinvention for the digital age. Marcus Miller composed, arranged, and played nearly every instrument, building sleek, synth-driven backdrops over which Davis's trumpet floats with regal authority. Named for Archbishop Desmond Tutu, the title track has a cinematic grandeur. The album won a Grammy and introduced Davis to an MTV-era audience while dividing jazz purists.

Doo-Bop

1992
Warner Bros. Rec. 1991 · Released posthumously Jun 1992
Davis's final album, released eight months after his death. A collaboration with hip-hop producer Easy Mo Bee that fuses Davis's trumpet over programmed beats and samples. A fitting coda from an artist who never stopped reaching toward the next sound.

Key Live Albums

1964–1982 · The Essential Concert Recordings

My Funny Valentine: Miles Davis in Concert

1965
Columbia Rec. Feb 12, 1964 · Philharmonic Hall, Lincoln Center
The ballad half of the legendary 1964 Lincoln Center concert. Davis's reading of the title tune is one of the most emotionally devastating trumpet performances ever recorded — fragile, searching, and utterly exposed. With the young Herbie Hancock and Tony Williams already pushing the rhythmic boundaries, this is the transitional quintet at its most intimate.
Personnel
Miles Davis (tp), George Coleman (ts), Herbie Hancock (p), Ron Carter (b), Tony Williams (d)
hard bop ballads live

'Four' & More: Recorded Live in Concert

1966
Columbia Rec. Feb 12, 1964 · Philharmonic Hall, Lincoln Center
The up-tempo counterpart to My Funny Valentine, from the same concert. The tempos are blistering — "So What" and "Walkin'" are taken at speeds that seem physically impossible. Tony Williams, at 18, drives the band with a ferocity that redefines what jazz drumming can be. One of the great live jazz documents, period.
Personnel
Miles Davis (tp), George Coleman (ts), Herbie Hancock (p), Ron Carter (b), Tony Williams (d)
hard bop post-bop live

Miles Davis at Fillmore

1970
In library Columbia Rec. Jun 17–20, 1970 · Fillmore East, NYC
Four nights at Bill Graham's rock palace, each edited into one LP side. Davis brought his electric band into rock's home turf, opening for Laura Nyro and the Steve Miller Band. With two keyboards (Corea and Jarrett), the sound is dense and hallucinatory. The music draws from In a Silent Way and Bitches Brew but takes on a rawer, more confrontational live energy.
Personnel
Miles Davis (tp), Steve Grossman (ss, ts), Chick Corea (el p), Keith Jarrett (org), Dave Holland (b, el b), Jack DeJohnette (d), Airto Moreira (perc)
jazz-rock fusion live

Live-Evil

1971
Owned Columbia Rec. Dec 1970 (live, Cellar Door) & studio sessions
A hybrid of scorching live recordings from the Cellar Door in Washington D.C. and studio tracks featuring Hermeto Pascoal's compositions. The Cellar Door material — with Gary Bartz, Keith Jarrett, and Michael Henderson's funk bass — captures the electric band at its most ferocious. Teo Macero's editing weaves the live and studio elements into a dark, exhilarating whole.

Studio: adds John McLaughlin (el g), Herbie Hancock (el p), Wayne Shorter (ss), Hermeto Pascoal (d, vcl), and others. Produced by Teo Macero.
jazz-rock funk live + studio

Agharta

1975
In library Columbia Rec. Feb 1, 1975 (afternoon) · Festival Hall, Osaka
The afternoon concert from Davis's final day of live performance before his retirement. A searing, dense wall of electric sound — Pete Cosey's distorted guitar, wah-wah trumpet, three-percussion rhythm — that anticipates industrial music and noise rock by a decade. Davis was in terrible physical pain during this period, and the music has a desperate, apocalyptic intensity.
Personnel
Miles Davis (tp, org), Sonny Fortune (as, ss, fl), Pete Cosey (el g, synth, perc), Reggie Lucas (el g), Michael Henderson (el b), Al Foster (d), James Mtume (perc, cga)

Pangaea

1976
In library Columbia Rec. Feb 1, 1975 (evening) · Festival Hall, Osaka
The evening concert from the same day as Agharta — Davis's final live performance before his six-year retirement. Even more intense and extreme than the afternoon set, with longer, more abstract passages and heavier distortion. Originally released only in Japan, it has become a cult classic and a touchstone for avant-garde rock musicians. The last word of Miles's electric period.
Personnel
Miles Davis (tp, org), Sonny Fortune (as, ss, fl), Pete Cosey (el g, synth, perc), Reggie Lucas (el g), Michael Henderson (el b), Al Foster (d), James Mtume (perc, cga)

We Want Miles

1982
Columbia Rec. Jun–Oct 1981 · Boston, NYC, Tokyo
The live document of Davis's triumphant 1981 comeback tour, compiled from three concerts. Won the Grammy for Best Jazz Instrumental Performance. Mike Stern's aggressive guitar and Marcus Miller's snapping electric bass give the band a contemporary edge. "Jean Pierre" — a simple, haunting melody — became Davis's signature encore piece for the rest of his life.
Personnel
Miles Davis (tp), Bill Evans (ss, ts, fl), Mike Stern (el g), Marcus Miller (el b), Al Foster (d), Mino Cinelu (perc)
