Ol’ Dirty Bastard

Russell Tyrone Jones — the Wu-Tang Clan’s unhinged, magnetic wild card, whose off-kilter vocal delivery, raw humor, and fearless disregard for convention made him one of hip-hop’s most singular voices. His solo work channeled the same chaotic energy that electrified the Clan, filtering RZA’s gritty production through a personality that defied every expectation.

Studio Albums

1995–1999 · Two releases on Elektra Records

Return to the 36 Chambers: The Dirty Version

1995
 🔈💻 Owned Elektra
ODB’s solo debut and one of the most unhinged records in hip-hop history. Built almost entirely on RZA’s murky, lo-fi production, the album showcased a delivery style unlike anything else in rap — half-sung, half-shouted, veering between menace and absurdist comedy. Certified platinum on the strength of singles that became street anthems in Brooklyn and beyond.

Nigga Please

1999
 🔈💻 Owned Elektra
The sophomore album arrived amid escalating legal troubles and found ODB working with an expanded roster of producers including The Neptunes and Irv Gotti alongside the familiar RZA and True Master. The result was slicker but no less unhinged, anchored by the massive Kelis-featuring single that became his biggest commercial hit and a defining track of late-’90s hip-hop.

Posthumous Releases

2005 · Two releases following ODB’s death on November 13, 2004

Osirus

2005
Sure Shot
A posthumous mixtape compiled from sessions recorded between January 2002 and December 2004, released barely two months after ODB’s death. Rougher and more raw than his official studio work, it captures a restless artist cycling through collaborators and styles, with DJ Premier’s production on several tracks lending structure to the chaos.

A Son Unique

2005
Dame Dash / Def Jam
Originally planned as ODB’s third studio album under Damon Dash’s label, it was shelved after his death and eventually released as a digital-only download. The production roster reads like a who’s-who of early-2000s hip-hop — Mark Ronson, DJ Premier, The Neptunes, RZA, and Rockwilder all contributed beats. Never physically released, it remains a tantalizing glimpse of where ODB’s career might have gone.
